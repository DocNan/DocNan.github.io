Langmuir Wave

Author: DocNan Date: August 22, 2018  22:41:50 Category: Physics

Langmuir wave could also be treated as electron acoustic/sound wave. In cold plasma it is just Langmuir oscillation that could not spread, in warm plasma it could propagate as a kind of longitudinal wave.

Langmuir oscillation

The physical model of Langmuir oscillation and wave is based on the assumption that the movement of ion can be neglected. Which means in the double fluid equation, only the equation for electrons are kept. (vi=0,pi=0v_i=0, p_i=0vi​=0,pi​=0) Compare with ion sound wave, could we treat Langmuir wave as “electron sound/acoustic wave” ? Langmuir oscillation and wave applies to the condition of plasma without external magnetic field.

double fluid equations

Langmuir oscillation treats the plasma as a double fluid. We can make some changes to the two fluid equations that describes the Langmuir oscillation.

Langmuir wave in hot plasma

The Languire oscillation in cold plasma can not propagate, since its group velocity equals to zero. But in the hot plasma condition, due to the existence of pressure. There exist Languire wave that can propagate in plasma. In hot plasma ∂pe∂x≠0\frac{\partial p_e}{\partial x}\neq 0∂x∂pe​​​=0. Thus the equations becomes:

∂n1∂t+n0∂ue∂x=0men0∂ue∂t=−∂pe∂x−en0E1∂E1∂x=en1ϵ0\begin{array}{l} \frac{\partial n_1}{\partial t}+n_0\frac{\partial u_e}{\partial x}=0\\ m_e n_0 \frac{\partial u_e}{\partial t}=-\frac{\partial p_e}{\partial x}-e n_0 E_1\\ \frac{\partial E_1}{\partial x}=\frac{e n_1}{\epsilon_0} \end{array} ∂t∂n1​​+n0​∂x∂ue​​=0me​n0​∂t∂ue​​=−∂x∂pe​​−en0​E1​∂x∂E1​​=ϵ0​en1​​​

But only the three equations are not enough, we should add a equations of static to make the equations close. Also we assume the state is adiatic ∂T1∂x=0\frac{\partial{T_1}}{\partial x}=0∂x∂T1​​=0. and equation of static is peneγ=constantp_e n_e^{\gamma}=constantpe​neγ​=constant and pe=neTep_e=n_e T_epe​=ne​Te​. From the 2 equations we can get: ∂pe∂x=γTe∂n1∂x\frac{\partial p_e}{\partial x}=\gamma Te\frac{\partial n_1}{\partial x}∂x∂pe​​=γTe∂x∂n1​​
Combine this relation and \refeq{Languir_wave_eq}, we can get: −me∂2n1∂t2=−γTe∂2n1∂x2+e2n0ϵ0n1-m_e\frac{\partial^2 n_1}{\partial t^2}=-\gamma T_e\frac{\partial^2 n_1}{\partial x^2}+\frac{e^2 n_0}{\epsilon_0}n_1−me​∂t2∂2n1​​=−γTe​∂x2∂2n1​​+ϵ0​e2n0​​n1​. Further we can get the dispersion relation of Langmuir wave:

ω2=e2n0meϵ0+γTemek2=ωpe2+γTemek2\omega^2=\frac{e^2 n_0}{m_e \epsilon_0}+\frac{\gamma T_e} {m_e}k^2=\omega^2_{pe}+\frac{\gamma T_e}{m_e}k^2 ω2=me​ϵ0​e2n0​​+me​γTe​​k2=ωpe2​+me​γTe​​k2

How could electrostatic perturbation exist alone?

Why for the Langmuir wave, there is only electric perturbation, no magnetic perturbation presents?
To explain this we will start from basic Maxwell equation.

∇×E⃗=−∂B⃗∂t∇×B⃗=μ0j⃗+μ0ϵ0∂E⃗∂t\nabla\times\vec{E} = -\frac{\partial \vec{B}}{\partial t}\\ \nabla\times\vec{B} = \mu_0\vec{j} + \mu_0\epsilon_0\frac{\partial \vec{E}}{\partial t} ∇×E=−∂t∂B​∇×B=μ0​j​+μ0​ϵ0​∂t∂E​

We make a replacement of the differential operator by applying the perturbation in this form:  ei(k⃗⋅r⃗−ωt)~e^{i(\vec{k}\cdot\vec{r}-\omega t)} ei(k⋅r−ωt), where we have the relation: ∇∼ik⃗\nabla \sim \vec{ik}∇∼ik, ∂∂t∼−iω\frac{\partial}{\partial t} \sim -i\omega∂t∂​∼−iω. Thus the above two equation becomes:

k⃗×E1⃗=−ωB1⃗k⃗×B1⃗=μ0j1⃗−μ0ϵ0ωE1⃗\begin{array}{l} \vec{k}\times\vec{E_1} = -\omega\vec{B_1}\\ \vec{k}\times\vec{B_1} = \mu_0\vec{j_1} - \mu_0\epsilon_0\omega\vec{E_1} \end{array} k×E1​​=−ωB1​​k×B1​​=μ0​j1​​−μ0​ϵ0​ωE1​​​

From equation we can see that if k⃗∥E1⃗\vec{k}\parallel\vec{E_1}k∥E1​​, then we could have B1⃗=0\vec{B_1} = 0B1​​=0. Which means even we have zero magnetic perturbation (B1⃗=0\vec{B_1} = 0B1​​=0), there could be non-zero electrostatic perturbation E1⃗≠0\vec{E_1} \neq 0E1​​​=0. But there is one key condition for the non-zero electrostatic perturbation to exist. There should be plasma.

Why do we need plasma here?
You can understand it by looking at the second equation.

k⃗×B1⃗=μ0j1⃗−μ0ϵ0ωE1⃗\vec{k}\times\vec{B_1} = \mu_0\vec{j_1} - \mu_0\epsilon_0\omega\vec{E_1} k×B1​​=μ0​j1​​−μ0​ϵ0​ωE1​​

If the magnetic perturbation is zero (B1⃗=0\vec{B_1} = 0B1​​=0), then we have k⃗×E1⃗=0\vec{k}\times\vec{E_1} = 0k×E1​​=0 (Since k⃗∥E1⃗\vec{k}\parallel\vec{E_1}k∥E1​​, there must be k⃗⊥B1⃗\vec{k}\perp\vec{B_1}k⊥B1​​. It is obvious that only when B1⃗=0\vec{B_1} = 0B1​​=0, will the term k⃗×B1⃗\vec{k}\times\vec{B_1}k×B1​​ be zero). Which lead to the equation: μ0j1⃗−μ0ϵ0ωE1⃗=0\mu_0\vec{j_1} - \mu_0\epsilon_0\omega\vec{E_1} = 0μ0​j1​​−μ0​ϵ0​ωE1​​=0, where means if you wish non-zero electrostatic perturbation E1⃗≠0\vec{E_1} \neq 0E1​​​=0, then there should be non-zero real plasma current j1⃗≠0\vec{j_1} \neq 0j1​​​=0 to balance it. Otherwise the magnetic perturbation will not be 0. So electrostatic perturbation/wave could only exist in plasma. In the vacuum, where we always have j1⃗=0\vec{j_1} = 0j1​​=0, only electromagnetic wave could exist.
