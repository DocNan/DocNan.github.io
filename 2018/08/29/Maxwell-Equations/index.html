Maxwell Equations

Author: DocNan Date: August 29, 2018  11:11:57

Maxwell equations are the fundamentals of electrodynamics[1].

Maxwell’s Equations in Vacuum

Notice, Gauss’s law for magnetism means that for magnetic field there is no magnetic charge like the case of electric charge. Which also means that for the magnetic field there is no source or drain. Where ϵ0\epsilon_0ϵ​0​​ and μ0\mu_0μ​0​​ are the permittivity and permeability of free space.

∬E⃗⋅ds⃗=1ϵ0∭ρdτ∬B⃗⋅ds⃗=0∮E⃗⋅dl⃗=−dΨdt∮B⃗⋅dl⃗=μ0I+μ0ϵ0∬∂E⃗∂t⋅ ds⃗\begin{array}{l} \iint\vec{E}\cdot d\vec{s}=\frac{1}{\epsilon_0}\iiint\rho d\tau\\ \iint\vec{B}\cdot d\vec{s}=0\\ \oint\vec{E}\cdot d\vec{l}=-\frac{d\Psi}{dt}\\ \oint\vec{B}\cdot d\vec{l}=\mu_0 I+\mu_0 \epsilon_0\iint\frac{\partial\vec{E}}{\partial t}\cdot\ d\vec{s} \end{array}​∬​E​⃗​​⋅d​s​⃗​​=​ϵ​0​​​​1​​∭ρdτ​∬​B​⃗​​⋅d​s​⃗​​=0​∮​E​⃗​​⋅d​l​⃗​​=−​dt​​dΨ​​​∮​B​⃗​​⋅d​l​⃗​​=μ​0​​I+μ​0​​ϵ​0​​∬​∂t​​∂​E​⃗​​​​⋅ d​s​⃗​​​​

Maxwell’s Equations in Dielectric Media

Electric polarization

For the magnetic fields in a dielectric media, there exist additional effect of electric polarization. Where the polarization vector P⃗=ϵ0χeE⃗\vec{P} = \epsilon_0 \chi_e \vec{E}​P​⃗​​=ϵ​0​​χ​e​​​E​⃗​​. And the displacement field vector is defined as: D⃗=P⃗+ϵ0E⃗=ϵ0(1+χe)E⃗=ϵ0ϵrE⃗=ϵE⃗\vec{D} = \vec{P} + \epsilon_0\vec{E} = \epsilon_0(1 + \chi_e)\vec{E} = \epsilon_0 \epsilon_r \vec{E} = \epsilon\vec{E}​D​⃗​​=​P​⃗​​+ϵ​0​​​E​⃗​​=ϵ​0​​(1+χ​e​​)​E​⃗​​=ϵ​0​​ϵ​r​​​E​⃗​​=ϵ​E​⃗​​, where: ϵ\epsilonϵ is the absolute permittivity of the media , ϵr=1+χe\epsilon_r = 1 + \chi_eϵ​r​​=1+χ​e​​ is the relative permittivity of the media, χe\chi_eχ​e​​ is the polarization rate of the media.

Magnetic polarization

For a media, with the presence of Magnetic field, similar to electric polarization, it will also suffer from a magnetic polarization. This means the magnetic field in the media will not be the original applied field strength. So we define the magnetizing field as: H⃗=1μB⃗=1μ0μrB⃗\vec{H} = \frac{1}{\mu}\vec{B} = \frac{1}{\mu_0\mu_r}\vec{B}​H​⃗​​=​μ​​1​​​B​⃗​​=​μ​0​​μ​r​​​​1​​​B​⃗​​

Differential Form

∇⋅D⃗=ρGauss′s Law∇⋅B⃗=0Gauss′s Law for Magnetism∇×E⃗=−∂B⃗∂tFaraday′s Law∇×H⃗=J⃗+∂D⃗∂tAmpere′s Law with Maxwell′s Addition}IV\left. \begin{array}{l l} \nabla\cdot\vec{D}=\rho & Gauss's\ Law\\ \nabla\cdot\vec{B}=0 & Gauss's\ Law\ for\ Magnetism\\ \nabla\times\vec{E}=-\frac{\partial\vec{B}}{\partial t} & Faraday's\ Law\\ \nabla\times\vec{H} = \vec{J} + \frac{\partial\vec{D}}{\partial t} & Ampere's\ Law\ with\ Maxwell's\ Addition \end{array} \right\} IV ​∇⋅​D​⃗​​=ρ​∇⋅​B​⃗​​=0​∇×​E​⃗​​=−​∂t​​∂​B​⃗​​​​​∇×​H​⃗​​=​J​⃗​​+​∂t​​∂​D​⃗​​​​​​​Gauss​′​​s Law​Gauss​′​​s Law for Magnetism​Faraday​′​​s Law​Ampere​′​​s Law with Maxwell​′​​s Addition​​​⎭​⎪​⎪​⎪​⎪​⎪​⎪​⎬​⎪​⎪​⎪​⎪​⎪​⎪​⎫​​IV

General case of Maxwell equations

∇⋅E⃗=ρϵ0Gauss′s Law∇⋅B⃗=0Gauss′s Law for Magnetism∇×E⃗=−∂B⃗∂tFaraday′s Law∇×B⃗=μ0J⃗+μ0ϵ0∂E⃗∂tAmpere′s Law with Maxwell′s Addition}V\left. \begin{array}{l l} \nabla\cdot\vec{E}=\frac{\rho}{\epsilon_0} & Gauss's\ Law\\ \nabla\cdot\vec{B}=0 & Gauss's\ Law\ for\ Magnetism\\ \nabla\times\vec{E}=-\frac{\partial\vec{B}}{\partial t} & Faraday's\ Law\\ \nabla\times\vec{B}=\mu_0\vec{J}+\mu_0\epsilon_0\frac{\partial\vec{E}}{\partial t} & Ampere's\ Law\ with\ Maxwell's\ Addition \end{array} \right\} V ​∇⋅​E​⃗​​=​ϵ​0​​​​ρ​​​∇⋅​B​⃗​​=0​∇×​E​⃗​​=−​∂t​​∂​B​⃗​​​​​∇×​B​⃗​​=μ​0​​​J​⃗​​+μ​0​​ϵ​0​​​∂t​​∂​E​⃗​​​​​​​Gauss​′​​s Law​Gauss​′​​s Law for Magnetism​Faraday​′​​s Law​Ampere​′​​s Law with Maxwell​′​​s Addition​​​⎭​⎪​⎪​⎪​⎪​⎪​⎪​⎪​⎬​⎪​⎪​⎪​⎪​⎪​⎪​⎪​⎫​​V

For equations V, if we consider ρ\rhoρ and j⃗\vec{j}​j​⃗​​ as the total charge density and current density (including the polarization effect) with ρe=ρef+ρeP\rho_e = \rho_{e_f} + \rho_{e_P}ρ​e​​=ρ​e​f​​​​+ρ​e​P​​​​ and j⃗=jf⃗+jP⃗+jM⃗\vec{j} = \vec{j_f} + \vec{j_P} + \vec{j_M}​j​⃗​​=​j​f​​​⃗​​+​j​P​​​⃗​​+​j​M​​​⃗​​ (where f,P,Mf, P, Mf,P,M each represent free, polarization and magneticsim). Then equations V is equivalent to equations IV for Maxwell’s equations in all medias. For example, plasma is a good conductor of electricity, thus ρP=0\rho_P = 0ρ​P​​=0, jP⃗=0\vec{j_P} = 0​j​P​​​⃗​​=0 but for dielectric media jP⃗≠0\vec{j_P} \neq 0​j​P​​​⃗​​≠0.

Why need displacement field vector D⃗\vec{D}​D​⃗​​

D⃗\vec{D}​D​⃗​​ is the introduced by Maxwell to the original Ampere’s law. But why should Maxwell introduce such a physical value to this equation [2]. First, we consider the Ampere’s law in the static condition ∮H⃗⋅dl⃗=I0\oint\vec{H}\cdot d\vec{l} = I_0∮​H​⃗​​⋅d​l​⃗​​=I​0​​. It is satisfied in all medias, but it fails under the case of changing field. When the electric field is changing in a system, eg: an electric circuit with an electric charger. We introduce two surfaces in the circuit where the two surfaces share the same Ampere loop LLL. S1S_1S​1​​ is placed across one cross section of the wire S2S_2S​2​​ is placed between the plates of the charger, Thus apply the original Ampere’s law, during the charing process of this circuit, we have: ∮H⃗⋅l⃗=I0=∬S1j1⃗⋅ds⃗≠0=∬S2j2⃗⋅ds⃗\oint\vec{H}\cdot\vec{l} = I_0 = \iint_{S_1}\vec{j_1}\cdot d\vec{s}\neq 0 = \iint_{S_2}\vec{j_2}\cdot d\vec{s}∮​H​⃗​​⋅​l​⃗​​=I​0​​=∬​S​1​​​​​j​1​​​⃗​​⋅d​s​⃗​​≠0=∬​S​2​​​​​j​2​​​⃗​​⋅d​s​⃗​​. Because in the wire current density j1⃗≠0\vec{j_1}\neq 0​j​1​​​⃗​​≠0 but between the plates of the charger current density j2⃗=0\vec{j_2} = 0​j​2​​​⃗​​=0. Thus Ampere’s law is no longer satisfied for the current changing circuit. To solve this problem, we look into the past of the Gauss’s law for charged particles ∬D⃗⋅ds⃗=q0\iint\vec{D}\cdot d\vec{s} = q_0∬​D​⃗​​⋅d​s​⃗​​=q​0​​. And considering the fact that current I=dqdtI = \frac{dq}{dt}I=​dt​​dq​​, then we have I0=ddt∬D⃗⋅s⃗I_0 = \frac{d}{dt}\iint\vec{D}\cdot\vec{s}I​0​​=​dt​​d​​∬​D​⃗​​⋅​s​⃗​​. If we put this part into the equation of Ampere’s law to makeup for no wire part of current. We have the Maxwell’s modified Ampere’s law as: ∮H⃗⋅dl⃗=I0+ddt∬D⃗⋅s⃗\oint\vec{H}\cdot d\vec{l} = I_0 + \frac{d}{dt}\iint\vec{D}\cdot\vec{s}∮​H​⃗​​⋅d​l​⃗​​=I​0​​+​dt​​d​​∬​D​⃗​​⋅​s​⃗​​. Convert this term to the derivative form with Stocks’s law we have: ∬∇×H⃗⋅ds⃗=∬j⃗⋅ds⃗+ddt∬D⃗⋅ds⃗=⇒∬(∇×H⃗−j⃗−∂∂tD⃗)⋅ds⃗=0\iint\nabla\times\vec{H}\cdot d\vec{s} = \iint\vec{j}\cdot d\vec{s} + \frac{d}{dt}\iint\vec{D}\cdot d\vec{s} = \Rightarrow \iint(\nabla\times\vec{H} - \vec{j} - \frac{\partial}{\partial t}\vec{D})\cdot d\vec{s} = 0∬∇×​H​⃗​​⋅d​s​⃗​​=∬​j​⃗​​⋅d​s​⃗​​+​dt​​d​​∬​D​⃗​​⋅d​s​⃗​​=⇒∬(∇×​H​⃗​​−​j​⃗​​−​∂t​​∂​​​D​⃗​​)⋅d​s​⃗​​=0. To let this term hold constant we must have ∇×H⃗=j⃗+ddtD⃗\nabla\times\vec{H} = \vec{j} + \frac{d}{dt}\vec{D}∇×​H​⃗​​=​j​⃗​​+​dt​​d​​​D​⃗​​. Thus the derivative of Maxwell modified Ampere’s law is satisfied for both static and time changing conditions.
