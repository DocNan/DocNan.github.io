Orbit Solutions

Author: DocNan Date: December 28, 2018  22:12:09 Category: Physics

Here summarize some orbits of motion that I encountered in life experience.

Orbit of double encircling motion

On Feb 28th, I met a problem in a chat group that why any point on a ball encircling a larger ball with half radius has an orbit of a straight line. After some calculation, I find this kind of complex motion can be explained with a super position of two circling motions as the above this shows:

As this figure shows, a small circle of radius r is circling around the larger circle with radius \(R = 2r\) on the outer side. However, strange thing happens as the point A on the small circle moving in a straight line along AA', when the smaller circle is encircling the larger circle.

\(r = R/2\)

A simpler relation can be draw from this motion that the length of curve \(l_{AB} = R\theta = R\Omega t = r\phi = r\omega t\), thus we have the relation between two angular velocity as: \(\frac{\omega}{\Omega} = \frac{R}{r} = 2\). However, if we look from the coordinate \(x'y'\), we can find that the angular velocity of the small circle is only half that of the original value as: \(\omega' = \frac{1}{2}\omega = \Omega\). And we know that only two parallel coordinates could be add up directly. Thus we can split the motion of point A on the small circle in two parts.

The first is the motion of the center of the small circle \(P(X,Y)\), taking the center of the larger circle O as the start point. The orbit equation is:

\[ \begin{array}{l} X = r cos(\Omega t)\\ Y = r sin(\Omega t) \end{array} \]

Second is the motion of the point \(A(x_A, y_A)\) around the small circle heat \(P(X, Y)\). Suppose the relative position of point A in the \(x'y'\) coordinates is \(A(x', y')\), then we have:

\[ \begin{array}{l} x' = r cos(-\omega' t)\\ y' = r sin(-\omega' t) \end{array} \]

Notice that the two circular motions are moving in the opposite direction, thus the two their angular velocity is different in sign (one positive, one negative). Where \(\omega' = \frac{1}{2}\omega = \Omega\), then the real motion of point A(x, y) in the 'xy' coordinates is the super position of the two motions as:

\[ \begin{array}{l} x = X + x' = r cos(\Omega t) + r cos(-\omega' t) = R cos(\Omega t)\\ y = Y + y' = r sin(\Omega t) + r sin(-\omega' t) = 0 \end{array} \]

From this relation we can clearly see that the point A(x, y) is moving on the x axis in a straight line.

If the radius of the small ball is not half of the radius of the larger ball. The trajectory of a point on the small ball will not be a straight line. There are two cases. However the angular velocity in the \(x'y'\) will be the same:

\(r < R/2\)

In this case, the motion equation becomes: \[ \begin{array}{l} x = X + x' = (R/2 + r)cos(\Omega t)\\ y = Y + y' = (R/2 - r)sin(\Omega t) \end{array} \] This the parameter equation of the ellipse \(\frac{x^2}{(R/2 + r)^2 } + \frac{y^2}{(R/2 - r)^2} = 1\)

\(r > R/2\)

In this case, \(R/2 - r < 0\). However, it can be easily seen that the orbit equation in the case has the same expression as the case for \(r < R/2\). Which is: \(\frac{x^2}{(r + R/2)^2 } + \frac{y^2}{(r - R/2)^2} = 1\).
