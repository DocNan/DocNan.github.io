Quantum Energy Spectrums

Author: DocNan Date: February 12, 2019  19:45:27

Don’t consider the quantum mechanics is how far from you. The beautiful colors you see in the world are actually light quantum beams that excited under the law of quantum mechanics. Here we will start from the very basic quantum concepts. Then we will discuss the isolated and continuous energy spectrums in quantum mechanics.

Matter wave

Is an electron a wave or a particle, that is a problem! The idea of matter wave (or called Quantum/de Broglie wave) was first introduced by Prof. Louis de Broglie. Where he proposed that actual particles/objects also have wave-like behaviors. This wave of real object is the so called “matter wave” or “de Broglie wave”.

Einstein’s relation for photon

Actually the idea of matter wave is an extension from the photon quantum theory first proposed by Einstein to explain the photoelectric effect [6].

de Broglie’s relation for particle

Extension to de Broglie relation as:

{ω=Eℏk⃗=p⃗ℏ(de Broglie′s relation for real particles)\left\{ \begin{array}{l} \omega = \frac{E}{\hbar}\\ \vec{k} = \frac{\vec{p}}{\hbar} \end{array} \right. (de\ Broglie's\ relation\ for\ real\ particles) ​⎩​⎪​⎨​⎪​⎧​​​ω=​ℏ​​E​​​​k​⃗​​=​ℏ​​​p​⃗​​​​​​(de Broglie​′​​s relation for real particles)

Where the reduced Planck constant is ℏ=h2π\hbar=\frac{h}{2\pi}ℏ=​2π​​h​​, hhh is the original Planck constant [5]. The angular velocity is ω=2πν\omega = 2\pi\nuω=2πν (frequency ν=f\nu = fν=f). Later, the wave property of real particles (electron, etc) were proved by many experiments[1] [2]. Here we note the matter wave function as Ψ(r⃗,t)\Psi(\vec{r}, t)Ψ(​r​⃗​​,t). It represents the probability of a particle appears at a certain space-time. However, the matter wave function Ψ\PsiΨ is a complex function, only its absolute value have the real meaning of probability. It also satisfies the conservation condition as: ∫ΨΨ∗dr⃗=1\int \Psi\Psi^*d\vec{r} = 1∫ΨΨ​∗​​d​r​⃗​​=1. However the conservation of probability only applies to the discrete energy spectrum of particles. For the continuous energy spectrum, the integration ∫ΨΨ∗\int\Psi\Psi^*∫ΨΨ​∗​​ is not conserved, because the probability function Ψ(x)\Psi(x)Ψ(x) still have finite value even at infinite location.

Uncertainty principle

If you rewrite the wave length equation of matter wave λ=hp\lambda=\frac{h}{p}λ=​p​​h​​as: λp=h\lambda p = hλp=h You will see a shadow of uncertainty principle. Heisenberg gives a more accurate definition of uncertainty principle as:

ΔxΔp>=1/2ℏ\Delta x\Delta p >= 1/2\hbar ΔxΔp>=1/2ℏ

Which means the product of particle position error and momentum error is big than a limited value. The accurate measurement of position (smaller Δx\Delta xΔx) always lead to bad measurement of momentum (bigger Δp\Delta pΔp). Many similar relations are also called uncertainty principle if they share the key idea of Heisenberg.

Schrödinger equation

In order to describe matter wave mathematically, Schrodinger introduced a wave equation, which is the later famous Schrodinger equation. The first concept must be cleared is that Schrödinger equation is not derived from any other basis. Like the Newton’s law, Schrodinger equation is the first principle basis for all the other derivations [3]. So what we need to do is just apply the Schrodinger equation in various conditions. The general form of Schrodinger equaton is:

iℏ∂Ψ∂t=H^Ψi\hbar\frac{\partial \Psi}{\partial t} = \hat{H}\Psi iℏ​∂t​​∂Ψ​​=​H​^​​Ψ

Where H^\hat{H}​H​^​​ is the Hamilton operator that determines the system energy [4].

Quantum energy spectrum

For a particle in an external potential field U(r⃗)U(\vec{r})U(​r​⃗​​), the Schrödinger equation is:

iℏ∂Ψ∂t=−ℏ22m∇2Ψ+U(r⃗)Ψi\hbar\frac{\partial\Psi}{\partial t} = -\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\nabla^2\Psi + U(\vec{r})\Psi iℏ​∂t​​∂Ψ​​=−​2m​​ℏ​2​​​​∇​2​​Ψ+U(​r​⃗​​)Ψ

Then we use this particle as an example to examine the property of continuous and discrete energy spectrum. Here we consider the case of free particle in space without external field (U=0U = 0U=0). Then the Schrodinger equation shall be:

For this equation, if the potential energy U do not consist time, then the general solution can be guessed to have this format: Ψ(r⃗,t)=ψE(r⃗)f(t)\Psi(\vec{r}, t) = \psi_E(\vec{r})f(t)Ψ(​r​⃗​​,t)=ψ​E​​(​r​⃗​​)f(t). The second equation that do not contain time explicitly is the energy eigen equation of matter waves. Which is usually rewritten as:

ℏ22m∇2ψ+(E−U)ψ=0\frac{\hbar^2}{2m}\nabla^2\psi + (E-U)\psi = 0 ​2m​​ℏ​2​​​​∇​2​​ψ+(E−U)ψ=0

Continuous energy spectrum

Mathematically, any value of energy E do relates to a solution to the above eigen equation of matter wave. Thus in the free space without confinement (potential U=0U = 0U=0), continuous energy spectrum is produced. However, with boundary confinement, only some discrete energy (eigen) values relate to meaningful solutions to the eigen equation of matter wave. Thus the discrete energy spectrum is produced.

Hydrogen atom energy spectrum

The case of Hydrogen atom’s energy spectrum involves solving the Schrodinger equation for the Coulomb potential U(r)=−e2rU(r) = \frac{-e^2}{r}U(r)=​r​​−e​2​​​​ in the 3D space. It is a bit complicated, however it could still be solved analytically. The details to solve this equation is not presented here. We just present some very important and interesting results. The discrete energy spectrum of Hydrogen atom by solving the Schrodinger equation are:

E=En=−μe42ℏ2n2=−e22an2,n=1,2,3,...E = E_n = -\frac{\mu e^4}{2\hbar^2 n^2} = -\frac{e^2}{2a n^2}, n = 1, 2, 3, ... E=E​n​​=−​2ℏ​2​​n​2​​​​μe​4​​​​=−​2an​2​​​​e​2​​​​,n=1,2,3,...

The related eigenfunction solution is: ψ(r,θ,ϕ)=Rnl(r)Ylm(θ,ϕ)\psi(r, \theta, \phi) = R_{nl}(r)Y_{lm}(\theta, \phi)ψ(r,θ,ϕ)=R​nl​​(r)Y​lm​​(θ,ϕ), the particular form of the function RRR and YYY are not to be presented here. From this we can see the once important energy levels hypothesis proposed by Bohr [6] to explain the radiation spectrum of Hydrorgen can be wonderfully derived by solving the Schrodinger equation. This is the power and beauty of math and physics.

Mystery of colors

{width=350px}

Here is an absorption and emission spectrum of Hydrogen atom [7]. From quantum mechanics we now know that lights (photons) are actually produced by the transmission of electrons between different energy levels on the atom’s discrete energy spectrum. The color of the light is determined by the wave length of the photon. And the photon energy is discretely determined by the atom energy spectrum. Thus it’s clear that the color of an object is determined by the law of quantum mechanics. You can see from the beautiful colors that quantum physics are actually not far from us.
